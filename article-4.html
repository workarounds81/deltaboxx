Secondary Market Volume Hits All-Time High Amid Liquidity Pressure

By the DeltaBoxx Research Team  ·  8 min read

The private equity secondary market has entered a new era. Q1 2025 secondary transaction volume reached $68 billion globally — a new quarterly record that surpassed the previous high set in Q3 2021 at the peak of the pandemic-era liquidity wave. Full-year 2025 volume is now projected to exceed $200 billion for the first time, a figure that would have seemed fantastical even five years ago when the entire secondary market was a fraction of that size. What is driving this extraordinary growth, and what does it mean for investors on both sides of these transactions?

The proximate cause is well understood: a sustained distribution drought. Buyout funds that made investments in 2019, 2020, and 2021 — at multiples that, in many cases, assumed a benign rate environment that subsequently evaporated — have been unable to exit at attractive prices. The result is an ageing portfolio overhang that has suppressed DPI ratios across the industry and left LPs with unfunded commitments to existing relationships while receiving fewer dollars back to recycle into new commitments. For LPs managing to strict allocation targets, this creates genuine pressure to find liquidity through the secondary market rather than waiting indefinitely for primary fund distributions.

The Rise of GP-Led Transactions

The most significant structural development in the secondary market is the ascendancy of GP-led transactions — now accounting for nearly 50% of total secondary volume, up from less than 20% a decade ago. In a GP-led secondary, rather than an LP selling their fund interest, the GP itself engineers a transaction that provides liquidity to existing LPs while continuing to own and manage specific portfolio assets.

The most common form is the continuation vehicle (CV): a GP identifies one or more high-quality portfolio companies it believes have significant remaining upside, transfers those assets into a new vehicle with a fresh investment period, and offers existing LPs the choice to roll their interest into the CV or take cash from new secondary investors who buy out the rolling LPs. Crucially, this structure allows GPs to hold their best assets longer without the arbitrary constraint of a ten-year fund lifecycle — a flexibility that many believe leads to better outcome optimisation for all parties.

Critics argue that GP-led transactions create conflicts of interest: the GP sets the price, selects the assets, and continues to collect management fees and carry on the retained portfolio. These concerns are valid and have prompted a cottage industry of independent valuation advisors, LP advisory committees, and secondary market intermediaries who provide price discovery and fairness opinions. The best-run GP-led processes are genuinely competitive, with multiple secondary buyers competing to acquire LP stakes at the highest price — effectively providing a market-clearing mechanism that protects LP interests.

LP-Led Volume: Motivated Sellers and Patient Buyers

Traditional LP-led secondary transactions — where a limited partner sells their interest in one or more funds — remain a large and important segment. The LP seller universe has evolved significantly. In earlier eras, sellers were predominantly motivated by distress: endowments facing liquidity crises, smaller LPs unable to fund capital calls, or institutions rebalancing after public market dislocations. Today's sellers are often highly sophisticated, using secondary sales as deliberate portfolio management tools rather than emergency exits.

Public pension funds in the US, UK, and Canada have become regular secondary sellers, using portfolio pruning exercises to reduce GP relationships from an unwieldy fifty or sixty to a more manageable core of twenty to twenty-five. Insurance companies are selling tail-end fund interests approaching the end of their investment periods, freeing up capital from assets that no longer meet their risk/return thresholds. And family offices are using secondary sales to rebalance between vintages, increasing exposure to more recent funds while monetising older positions.

On the buy side, dedicated secondary funds — including vehicles raised by Lexington Partners, Ardian, Hamilton Lane, and Blackstone's Strategic Partners — are deploying record capital into a market that offers attractive pricing, diversification, and a clearer J-curve than primary fund investing. Secondary buyers typically acquire LP interests at a discount to NAV, providing a built-in margin of safety, and they have visibility into the underlying portfolio companies that primary fund investors at the time of commitment did not have.

Pricing Dynamics and Discount Compression

One of the most watched indicators in the secondary market is the average bid-ask spread — the gap between what sellers want and what buyers will pay. During the dislocation of 2022, discounts to NAV widened significantly as buyers repriced for higher rates and lower comparable public company valuations. By early 2025, discounts have partially compressed as NAVs have been written down to reflect market realities and competition among secondary buyers has intensified.

Buyout fund interests are now trading at approximately 88-92 cents on the dollar on average, with high-quality assets from top-quartile managers trading at or above par. Venture capital portfolio interests — many still carrying inflated 2021 valuations — remain more deeply discounted, often clearing at 60-75 cents depending on the vintage and underlying company quality. Real estate and infrastructure secondaries are trading at a premium to other asset classes given the quality of cash flows and inflation-linkage of the underlying assets.

The DeltaBoxx View

The secondary market's evolution from a niche liquidity mechanism to a mainstream asset allocation tool represents a coming-of-age for private capital markets. For DeltaBoxx and our LP community, the liquid secondary market serves as an important risk management tool — providing optionality that partially offsets the illiquidity premium we accept when committing capital to private funds. We actively monitor secondary market pricing as a real-time indicator of where institutional investors are marking private assets, using it to inform our own portfolio construction and pacing decisions. The record volume of 2025 is not a symptom of market stress — it is a sign of market maturation, and we view it as structurally positive for the long-term health of private capital ecosystems globally.
