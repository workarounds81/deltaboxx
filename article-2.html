AI Data Centre Demand Fuels Record Infrastructure Deal Flow

By the DeltaBoxx Research Team  ·  9 min read

The artificial intelligence revolution is not only reshaping software — it is fundamentally transforming the physical infrastructure of the global economy. Private equity's infrastructure arm has become one of the most active participants in this transformation, with deal values in the data centre, power generation, and digital connectivity sectors rising 34% in 2025 versus the prior year. The numbers are staggering: estimates suggest that meeting projected AI compute demand through 2030 will require over $2 trillion in new infrastructure investment globally. Private capital is positioning itself at the centre of that opportunity.

Data centres have emerged as the decade's defining infrastructure asset class. Hyperscale facilities — those exceeding 100 megawatts of IT load — are being developed or acquired at a pace that would have seemed implausible five years ago. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta are collectively committing hundreds of billions of dollars to compute capacity expansion, and a substantial portion of that capacity is being sourced from, or co-developed with, private equity-backed operators. The colocation market, once considered a mature and modestly growing segment, is experiencing the kind of demand shock typically reserved for nascent asset classes.

The Power Problem

But data centres are only one layer of the opportunity stack. The AI infrastructure buildout has exposed a profound bottleneck: power. Training a single frontier large language model can consume as much electricity as a small city uses in a year. Inference workloads — running AI models at scale once trained — are cumulatively even more energy-intensive. The result is an extraordinary surge in demand for power generation capacity that is reliable, low-latency, and ideally clean.

Infrastructure PE managers are responding by acquiring and developing a wide range of power assets: gas peaker plants that can respond to sudden demand spikes, nuclear facilities that can provide baseload zero-carbon power, utility-scale solar and wind projects co-located near data centre campuses, and even early-stage advanced nuclear developers. The latter category — small modular reactors and fusion energy startups — were historically dismissed as venture bets with no infrastructure relevance; today, they are being courted by infrastructure GPs as potential anchor assets in decade-long power supply agreements.

The geographic dimension of this power constraint is acute in specific markets. Northern Virginia — home to the world's highest concentration of data centres — is facing genuine power availability limits that are forcing developers to look at alternative locations. The US Midwest, with its abundant wind resources and aging industrial power infrastructure, is emerging as a new data centre hub. Europe faces its own constraints, particularly in the Netherlands and Ireland where grid operators have instituted moratoriums on new large-scale connections.

Fibre and Connectivity Infrastructure

Connectivity infrastructure — fibre optic networks, subsea cables, and wireless tower portfolios — is experiencing a parallel boom. AI applications require not just raw compute power but ultra-low-latency, high-bandwidth connectivity between data centres, edge compute nodes, and end users. This has renewed investment interest in fibre-to-the-premise rollouts in suburban and rural markets that were previously considered uneconomic, as well as driving extraordinary demand for new subsea cable capacity connecting AI compute hubs across continents.

Private equity has been an active consolidator in the tower sector for over a decade, but the AI era is adding new dimensions to tower economics. Edge compute — processing data at or near the network edge rather than routing everything to centralised hyperscale facilities — is creating demand for densified small cell networks attached to existing tower infrastructure. The tower operators who have already built relationships with major mobile carriers are well positioned to capture this upgrade cycle.

Valuation Dynamics and Return Expectations

The surge in demand for AI infrastructure assets has predictably compressed cap rates and entry multiples. Core-plus data centre assets in tier-one markets are trading at EBITDA multiples that would have been considered absurd in 2020. Infrastructure managers who built positions early — some as far back as 2017 and 2018 — are sitting on extraordinary unrealised gains and facing the challenge of whether to hold for continued growth or crystallise returns in a hot exit market.

For new capital being deployed today, the return math is more nuanced. The most sophisticated managers are differentiating between: core data centre assets with long-term hyperscaler leases (lower risk, lower return, significant cap rate compression already baked in); value-add opportunities involving older facilities requiring power and cooling upgrades (higher risk, higher return, operational expertise required); and development plays involving ground-up construction in emerging markets where demand signals are clear but execution risk is meaningful. DeltaBoxx's framework favours the middle category — value-add repositioning of existing infrastructure — as offering the most attractive risk-adjusted profile in the current environment.

Geopolitical Dimensions

AI infrastructure is not just a financial asset class — it is a geopolitical one. Governments across the US, EU, and major Asian economies have identified compute sovereignty as a national security priority. The EU AI Act, US export controls on advanced semiconductors, and China's parallel push to build domestic AI infrastructure are all creating both constraints and opportunities for private capital.

Constraints include increasing scrutiny of cross-border infrastructure transactions involving strategic AI assets, potential restrictions on foreign LP participation in certain sensitive infrastructure funds, and the risk that regulatory fragmentation increases operational complexity for globally diversified platforms. Opportunities include government co-investment programs, offtake agreements, and regulatory support for domestic data centre development in markets seeking to establish sovereign compute capacity.

The DeltaBoxx View

We are at an inflection point in the history of infrastructure as an asset class. The AI compute buildout is not a cyclical phenomenon — it is a structural shift that will continue to drive capital deployment for a decade or more. DeltaBoxx's investment thesis places digital infrastructure at the core of our portfolio construction, recognising that the firms building and owning the physical substrate of AI will capture value at a scale that rivals the software platforms running on top of them. The pick-and-shovel opportunity in AI infrastructure is, in our view, the most compelling infrastructure investment theme of the current decade, and one we expect to actively position across our portfolio throughout 2025 and beyond.
