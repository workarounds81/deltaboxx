Pre-IPO Tech Stakes Draw Renewed Interest From Family Offices

By the DeltaBoxx Research Team  ·  8 min read

As the IPO window shows signs of reopening after a prolonged closure, family offices and private wealth platforms are moving aggressively to secure pre-IPO allocations in late-stage technology companies. The competition for these stakes is intense — and for good reason. The cohort of private technology companies currently approaching the public markets includes some of the most consequential businesses of the decade, spanning artificial intelligence infrastructure, autonomous systems, defence technology, and climate infrastructure. Investors who can access these companies before their public debut stand to capture the most significant appreciation in the value creation curve.

The trend is quantifiable. Data from multiple prime brokerage platforms shows that family office allocations to private technology companies increased by 42% in 2024 versus 2023, with a further acceleration expected in 2025. Deals that previously went exclusively to top-tier venture capital funds or large sovereign wealth funds are increasingly being opened to family offices that can move quickly, write meaningful cheques, and offer strategic value beyond capital — whether through operating expertise, geographic relationships, or distribution networks.

The Three Sectors Driving Demand

Artificial Intelligence. No theme dominates the pre-IPO conversation more than AI. A generation of companies building foundation models, AI infrastructure, and AI-native applications across every vertical has emerged over the past three years, and the most advanced cohort is now approaching the scale and revenue predictability that public markets demand. Investors are focused not merely on the model developers — a category where valuation has become extremely challenging to justify — but on the enabling layer: inference infrastructure, AI safety tooling, enterprise deployment platforms, and vertical AI applications in healthcare, legal services, financial analysis, and industrial automation. These businesses often have more defensible revenue models and more legible paths to profitability than their foundation model counterparts.

Defence Technology. The geopolitical environment of the past three years has fundamentally reoriented government procurement toward commercially developed defence technology. Autonomous drones, electronic warfare systems, satellite communications, cybersecurity platforms, and AI-powered intelligence analysis tools are all areas where venture-backed companies have demonstrated capabilities that exceed those of traditional defence prime contractors — at a fraction of the cost and development time. This has translated into an extraordinary pipeline of defence-tech companies with multi-year government contracts, predictable revenue, and complex barriers to competition. Family offices with an appetite for the regulatory complexity of the defence sector are finding compelling pre-IPO opportunities in a cohort that was largely invisible to private capital five years ago.

Climate Infrastructure. The energy transition represents one of the largest capital deployment opportunities in economic history, and the companies building the enabling technology — battery storage, green hydrogen production, grid management software, carbon capture systems, and EV charging infrastructure — are reaching stages of commercial maturity that make them viable IPO candidates. The Inflation Reduction Act in the US, REPowerEU in Europe, and parallel national programs in Asia have created a subsidy and procurement environment that dramatically de-risks these businesses' revenue models, making them attractive to both public market investors and the family offices seeking pre-IPO exposure.

Access and Information Asymmetry

The pre-IPO market is not democratised. Access remains highly stratified, with the most attractive deals going first to the incumbent venture funds who led earlier rounds, then to sovereign wealth funds with the relationship capital and cheque sizes to demand allocations, and finally to a select group of family offices and strategic investors who have built reputations as value-added shareholders. Breaking into this hierarchy requires either exceptional speed and flexibility, genuine strategic value to the company, or access through a trusted intermediary with relationships at the company or its existing investors.

Information asymmetry is profound. Public market investors in the same company post-IPO will have access to audited financials, SEC filings, and analyst coverage. Pre-IPO investors are typically working from investor presentations, limited management team access, and whatever primary research they can conduct on the market, technology, and competitive landscape. The investors who consistently generate alpha in the pre-IPO market are those who have built deep sector expertise — often by hiring former operators or investing in adjacent public market companies — that enables them to evaluate management quality, technology defensibility, and market size with genuine precision rather than informed guesswork.

The Lock-Up and Liquidity Challenge

Pre-IPO investing carries inherent liquidity risk that family offices must carefully underwrite. Even when an IPO occurs on the anticipated timeline, most pre-IPO investors face lock-up periods of 180 days post-listing during which they cannot sell. In a volatile market, a 180-day lock-up period can turn a strong IPO gain into a flat or negative return — as many 2021 vintage investors discovered when SPACs and high-growth tech companies collapsed after initial pops.

Family offices are managing this risk through several strategies: staggering pre-IPO commitments across multiple companies and sectors to avoid correlated liquidity events; negotiating registration rights that accelerate their ability to sell post-lock-up; and using OTC secondary markets for private company shares — platforms like Forge Global and Nasdaq Private Market — to partially monetise positions prior to IPO if pricing becomes attractive.
