Global PE Fundraising Rebounds as LPs Return to Alternatives

By the DeltaBoxx Research Team  ·  8 min read

After eighteen months of hesitation, the private equity industry is experiencing a meaningful resurgence. First-half 2025 global PE fundraising reached $420 billion — an 18% increase year-on-year — signalling a decisive return of limited partner conviction to the asset class. The rebound is broad-based, touching buyout, infrastructure, private credit, and growth equity strategies, though it is not without nuance. GPs who can demonstrate consistent DPI (distributions to paid-in capital) are winning disproportionately; those who cannot are being quietly sidelined.

The data tells a compelling story. According to aggregated LP survey data compiled by DeltaBoxx's research team, 74% of institutional allocators increased or maintained their target allocation to private equity through 2025, reversing the cautious posture of 2023 and early 2024. Sovereign wealth funds — particularly those from the Gulf Cooperation Council, Singapore, and Norway — have been among the most aggressive re-engagers, drawn by both attractive vintage year entry points and the structural need to generate returns above their long-term actuarial assumptions.

What Changed?

Three macro forces converged to restore confidence. First, the interest rate cycle peaked and began its gradual descent. With central banks in the US, EU, and UK all moving toward accommodation, the discount rates that had brutally compressed private asset valuations began to ease. Deal activity, which had seized up in 2023 as buyers and sellers could not bridge the valuation gap, is once again flowing — KPMG data shows PE deal volume in Q1 2025 was up 28% versus Q1 2024.

Second, the exit markets cracked open. After two years of near-paralysis in IPO markets and a subdued M&A environment, strategic acquirers and public markets reappeared as viable exit channels. Several high-profile PE-backed IPOs in the technology and healthcare sectors — all completing above their offering price — rebuilt LP confidence that the illiquidity premium is genuinely being realised, not simply deferred indefinitely.

Third, and perhaps most importantly, the narrative around private equity evolved. The framing shifted from "PE is a source of systemic risk" to "PE is a critical funding mechanism for the infrastructure, technology, and industrial transformation the global economy urgently needs." Policymakers and institutional boards began viewing private capital not as an alternative but as a necessary complement to public market portfolios.

Where the Capital Is Going

Not all PE strategies are benefiting equally. Infrastructure-focused funds are capturing the largest share of incremental capital, reflecting both the secular demand for energy transition assets and the rise of AI-driven data centre investment. Buyout mega-funds — those raising above $10 billion — have also performed strongly, leveraging brand recognition and institutional relationships to close efficiently. Mid-market buyout, historically the most consistent performer on a risk-adjusted basis, is seeing growing competition from family offices and sovereign funds who have built direct investing capabilities and are bypassing GPs entirely for certain deal types.

Private credit remains the standout growth story. As banks retreated from leveraged lending in the wake of 2023 regulatory tightening, direct lending funds filled the void and captured persistently attractive spreads. Several large PE managers — including platforms with over $100 billion in AUM — have seen their private credit AUM surpass their buyout AUM for the first time, a structural shift that would have been unthinkable a decade ago.

The LP Perspective

For limited partners, the recalibration has been both strategic and tactical. On the strategic side, many LPs used the 2023–2024 fundraising slowdown as an opportunity to consolidate their manager rosters. Rather than being spread across forty or fifty GP relationships, leading LPs are now concentrating commitments with a core group of fifteen to twenty preferred partners. This "barbell" approach — anchored by mega-fund relationships at one end and specialist niche funds at the other — reflects a more sophisticated understanding of where alpha genuinely accrues within private markets.

Tactically, LPs have become far more disciplined about pacing. The denominator effect of 2022 — when public equity market declines caused PE allocations to balloon as a percentage of total portfolios — left many institutions technically over-allocated for two full years. Having lived through that experience, treasury teams and investment committees are now building far more conservative pacing models with explicit buffers for market volatility.

Co-investment appetite is also at an all-time high. LPs are demanding co-investment rights as part of their fund commitment negotiations, seeking to deploy additional capital at lower fee structures into deals they already know from their primary fund exposure. The most sophisticated LPs — Canada's CPPIB, Australia's Future Fund, and select US endowments — have built teams that can evaluate and execute co-investments with a two-week turnaround, making them genuinely competitive with other institutional co-investors.

Emerging Markets and New Frontiers

One underappreciated dimension of the 2025 fundraising rebound is the growing role of emerging market PE. Southeast Asia, India, and select African markets are attracting increasing LP attention as domestic consumption growth, demographic tailwinds, and technology adoption curves combine to create compelling investment theses. India in particular is seeing extraordinary momentum: GDP growth above 6.5%, a maturing public capital market infrastructure, and a vibrant startup ecosystem are making it a consensus overweight for global PE allocators.

The Gulf region is simultaneously maturing from a source of capital into a destination for capital. Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 transformation agenda, the UAE's regulatory modernisation, and Qatar's post-World Cup investment drive are all creating genuine PE opportunities in sectors ranging from healthcare to logistics to financial services. Several global managers have opened regional offices in Riyadh and Abu Dhabi not merely to cultivate LP relationships but to source and execute deals in markets they increasingly view as structurally compelling.

The DeltaBoxx View

At DeltaBoxx, we view the 2025 PE fundraising rebound as a confirmation of our long-held thesis: that patient, research-driven capital deployed at the intersection of traditional private markets and next-generation technology infrastructure will generate superior risk-adjusted returns over a decade-plus horizon. We have observed this renaissance not just in data but in the conversations we have with our LP community — the quality of questions is better, the patience for complexity is greater, and the willingness to commit capital to less-liquid strategies for the right return profile is stronger than at any point since 2019.

The challenges ahead are real. Valuation discipline must be maintained even as deal competition intensifies. The carry ecosystem faces continuing scrutiny on fee structures. And the long-term question of whether private equity can deliver genuine alpha net-of-fees — rather than simply leverage and illiquidity premium — remains one that the industry must answer through performance, not marketing. But the direction of travel is clear: private capital is not just recovering. It is reshaping the global investment landscape for a generation.
